KNAVE

article · 697 chars · lineage ↗ · page image at NLS ↗

KNAVE, an old Saxon word, which had at first a sense of simplicity and innocence, for it signified a boy: Sax. enapa, whence a knave child, i. e. a boy, distinguished from a girl, in several old writers; afterwards it was taken for a servant boy, and at length for any servant man. Also it was applied to a minister or officer that bore the shield or weapon of his superior; as field knapa, whom the Latins call armiger, and the French escuyer, 14 Edw. III. c. 3. And it was sometimes of old made use of as a titular addition; as Joannes C. filius Wilhelmi C. de Derby, knave, &c. 22 Hen. VIII.

Knaveship VII. c. 37. The word is now perverted to the hardest meaning, viz. a false deceitful fellow.
