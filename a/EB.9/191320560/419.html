ERIDANUS

ERIDANUS, in Ancient Geography, a river of Attica, falling into the Ilissus.—Another Eridanus, the more ancient name of the Padus, an appellation ascribed by Pliny to the Greeks; followed in this by Virgil. It rises in Mount Vesuvius, in the Alpes Cottiae, and dividing the Cisalpine Gaul into the Cispadana and Transpadana, and swelled on each hand with no inconsiderable rivers from the other Alps and the Apennine, falls at seven mouths into the Adriatic. Famous in mythology, from the story of Phaëton; whose sisters, the Helides, were here changed into poplars, according to Ovid.
