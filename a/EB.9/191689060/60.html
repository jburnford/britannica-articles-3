PATAECI, in Mythology, images of gods which the Phœnicians carried on the prows of their gallees. Herodotus, lib. iv. calls them paluxes. The word is Phœnician, and derived from pethica, i. e. titular. See Bocchart's Chanaan, lib. ii. cap. 3. But Scaliger does not agree. Morin derives it from palux, monkey, this animal having been an object of worship among the Egyptians, and hence might have been honoured by their

Mr Elmer has observed, that Herodotus does not call the patagoni gods; but that they obtained this dignity from the liberality of Hefychius and Suidas, and other ancient lexicographers, who placed them at the stern of ships; whereas Herodotus placed them at the prow. Sealiger, Bochart, and Selden, have taken some pains about this subject.—M. Morin has also given us a learned dissertation on this head in the Memoires de l'Acad. des Inscrip. et Belles Lettres, tom. i.; but Mr Elmer thinks it defective in point of evidence.
