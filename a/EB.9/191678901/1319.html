ZIMENT-WATER, COPPER-WATER, the name by which some have called water found in places where there are copper-mines, which is impregnated with particles of that metal.

The most famous spring of this kind is about a mile distant from Newfohl in Hungary, in the great copper-mine called by the Germans henngrund. The water in this mine is found at different depths, and is received into basins, for the purpose of separating the copper from it: in some of these it is much more saturated with this metal than in others, and will make the supposed change of iron into that metal much sooner. The most common pieces of iron used in the experiments are horseshoes, nails, and the like; and they are found very little altered in shape, after the operation, except that their surfaces are more raised. The water appears greenish in the basin, where it stands; but if a glass of it be taken up, it looks clear as crystal: it has no smell, but a strong vitriolic asstringent taste, inasmuch that

the lips and tongue are blistered and scorched upon tasting it.
