CORFU

CORFU, an island in the Ionian sea, at the mouth of the gulf of Venice, formerly called Coreyra and Phœacia, famous for the gardens of Alcinoos. It belongs at present to the Venetians; and forms the bulwark of Christendom against the Turks, who have often attempted to reduce it, but without success. It is well fortified, and has 50 castles; and the number of the inhabitants is said to be about 50,000. The inhabitants are of the Greek church; and the Venetians send them a governor and magistrates, which are changed every two years. The soil is very fruitful, and produces a great deal of wine, olives, and several other fruits, particularly figs, which are exceedingly good. The chief city is likewise called Corfa: see the following article.
