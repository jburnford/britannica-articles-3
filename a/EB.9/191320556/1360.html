CATARACT

article · 887 chars · lineage ↗ · page image at NLS ↗

CATARACT, in Hydrography, a precipice in the channel of a river, caused by rocks, or other obstacles, stopping the course of the stream, from whence the water falls with greater noise and impetuosity. The word comes from καταρραξω, "I tumble down with violence;" compounded of κατα, "down," and ρᾶξω, dejiro, "I throw down."—Such are the cataracts of the Nile, the Danube, Rhine, &c. In that of Niagara, the perpendicular fall of the water is 137 feet; and in that of Pifil Rhaadr, in North Wales, the fall of water is near 240 feet from the mountain to the lower pool.

Strabo calls that a cataracl which we call a cascade; and what we call a cataracl, the ancients usually called a catadupa. Herminius has an express dissertation, "De admirandis mundi Cataraclis supra et subterraneis," where he uses the word in a new sense; signifying, by cataracl, any violent motion of the elements.
