ARPAGIUS

article · 943 chars · lineage ↗ · page image at NLS ↗

ARPAGIUS, or HARPAGIUS, among the ancients, a person who died in the cradle, at least in early youth. The word is formed from the Greek αρπαγαι, I snatch. The Romans made no funerals for their arpagii. They neither burnt their bodies, nor made tombs, monuments, or epitaphs for them; which occasioned Juvenal to say,

VOL. II. PART II.

Terra clauditur infans
Et minor igne rogi.

In after times it became the custom to burn such as had lived to the age of 40 days, and had cut any teeth; and these they called Αρπαγαι, or Αρπαγαιτι, q. d. rapiti, ravished. The usage seems to have been borrowed from the Greeks; among whom, Eustathius assures us, it was the custom never to bury their children either by night or full day, but at the first appearance of the morning; and that they did not call their departure by the name of death, but by a softer appellation, Ημετερην, importing that they were ravished by Aurora, or taken away to her embraces.
