THE METROPOLIS.

The rapid increase of the population in the suburbs, since the beginning of the last century, shows how rapidly the metropolis increases in extent, although its population has not increased so fast as that of the nation at large. In 1700, the metropolis contained almost an eighth part of the inhabitants of England and Wales; in 1750 and in 1801, about a tenth part; but in 1811 and at present, only about a twelfth part.

Objections might be made to the propriety of the limits of the metropolis as herein assumed, and it may therefore be right to add, that the population of all the parishes, of which the churches are situate within eight miles rectilinear around St Paul's Cathedral (including the before mentioned addition of one twenty-fifth part), would, in 1801, have amounted to 1,031,500;—in 1811, to 1,220,200;—and in 1821, to no less than 1,481,500; without including the parish of Woolwich, which contains 17,008 additional.

N. B. The population ascribed to the city of Paris is comprised within a district of full such an extent.

The arrivals of coasting vessels in the port of London may be estimated to have been about 5500, in the year 1700;—6400, in the year 1750;—10,400, in 1801;—13,500, in 1811; and at about 17,000 per annum, since the year 1817. This produces a constant, though fluctuating accession to the population, to a larger amount than elsewhere, and consequently, a twenty-fifth, instead of a fiftieth part, is to be added to form a comparison with the Parish Register Returns in the General Abstract. With this addition, the metropolis, in the year 1801, contained about 900,000;—in 1811, about 1,050,000;—and in 1821, about 1,274,800 inhabitants. The totals would then be, in
